The journey that culminated in this paper started with a simple observation of our Ph.D. student Naoto several years ago. Naoto observed something peculiar in his aggregation experiments on superoxide dismutase 1.

You might just have discarded his observations as "poor reproducibility" or "poor handling of the sample". But no! Naoto was sure that something with the experimental setup had triggered faster protein aggregation in some cases than in other cases.

About five years, a lot of experiments, simulations, and literature study later, we finally began to understand what happened. Anyway, detailed report to follow soon after publication is finalized!

What is biomolecular dynamics?

Hey look! This protein is moving along another protein! That's kind of interesting. Why does it do that? Does the molecule have a purpose? But it's just a molecule, composed of atoms and governed by the rules of the tiny world of quantum mechanics. Why does it have a goal and knows where to walk ... ?

We enjoy researching things like these: not just static structures of proteins, but the dynamic aspects of protein structure ...

We enjoy thinking about the dynamic aspects of molecular structure ...

... and what these fluctuations imply for physiology.

Flows, crowding, and even normal "Brownian" motion at 310 K gives life highly dynamic aspects that cannot be understood by static snapshot structures.

All of this could be summarized in the question: how does life and its processes change with time (d/dt)?

Lewis Kay and Reid Alderson have recently written a nice review in Cell which summarizes our scientific field (what do we mean by biomolecular dynamics?) very nicely. Please check Fig. 6 of their review for a visual impact, I will just state the main points here:

Examples of biomolecular dynamics

  • Ligand binding (small molecules or macromolecules)
  • Allosteric interactions
  • Determination of molecular structures (proteins, DNA, RNA, etc.)
  • Folding
  • Detection of low-population "minor" (= almost "invisible" states)
  • Mechanisms (e.g. enzymatic reactions, amyloid fibril formation, ...)
  • Dynamic motions (domain motions, local motions, smaller fluctuations, ...; e.g. flip-out of bases of DNA etc.)
  • Biochemistry of unstructured regions: domain linkers and intrinsically disordered proteins
  • Weak, transient, non-specific interactions
